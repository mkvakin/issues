# She: Psychological Analysis from the 2007 + 2009 Evaluations

> **Methodological framing**: This analysis is built entirely on [elizabeth_engler_extracts.md](elizabeth_engler_extracts.md), which compiles every she-attributed statement from two independent professional evaluations (Wilbourn Psy.D., Colorado, 2007; Gutierrez Ph.D., California, 2009). It does not draw on later events, on any third-party behavioral account from a subsequent relationship, or on any information that postdates September 2009. The goal is to see what these two evaluations, taken on their own, support as a clinical picture of her at ages 32-35.

> **What this analysis is not**: It is not a diagnosis. It is not a prediction beyond the 2009 horizon. It does not attempt to reconcile this picture with any later observations.

---

## 1. The evidentiary base

The picture is unusually well-anchored for an analysis of this kind. Two licensed psychologists in two US states, working two years apart with no professional contact between them (Gutierrez references the Wilbourn report through court records, not personal communication), administered overlapping standardized batteries (MMPI-2/II, MCMI-III, 16PF, PSI, PAM), conducted home visits, observed parent-child interaction, and interviewed an aggregate of more than fifteen collateral sources covering both sides of the family, daycare providers, friends of each party, and prior treating clinicians.

The two evaluations converge on a stable, recognizable clinical picture. They diverge mainly on tone (Wilbourn was somewhat more optimistic about her parenting; Gutierrez was more concerned) and on degree of formal coding (Wilbourn coded Axis II; Gutierrez did not formally Axis-code but described the same pattern in clinical narrative). This convergence under independent observation is itself a finding: the pattern is sufficiently stable across time, location, and observer to be characterological rather than situational.

## 2. Behavioral pattern summary

The behaviors documented in 2007-2009 cluster into six coherent domains.

### A. Image management and approval-seeking under defensive control
The most consistent finding across both batteries is defensiveness. The MMPI-2 in 2007 was invalidated by it: "rigid defensiveness resulted in an underestimate of her problems"; "she appears quite unwilling or unable to view herself psychologically and has little insight into her behavior." Wilbourn's MCMI-III: "preoccupied with approval, frequently conforms to social expectations of others, particularly those with status or authority"; "social affability may help to cloak a defensiveness about admitting psychological problems"; "moderately defensive about herself, both denying personal faults and presenting an idealized self image." Gutierrez 2009: "described her experiences growing up in glowing terms"; "vague and dismissive when discussing her emotional responses"; "tended to emphasize and embellish her virtues." This is a stable, structural feature, not a state-dependent reaction to the testing context.

### B. Manipulation and rule-bending toward authority figures
Both evaluators document that she treats institutional rules and authority structures as constraints to be worked around when they do not favor her position. Gutierrez: "considers herself outside of the rules which apply to others (such as knocking on a door which says please do not knock)"; "demanded the evaluator accommodate Russian-language testing"; "manipulative and intrusive in her interactions with others." Multiple concrete instances: asking a daycare provider to falsify hours and to sign a statement that more money had been paid than actually had been (with the offer of money in exchange); withholding payment to that provider when refused; switching the child's pediatrician without informing the father; submitting documents past the deadline; selectively submitting "reams of copied emails." Wilbourn's forensic finding put it directly: "capable of engaging in parental alienation, and then calling it something else."

### C. Use of legal and institutional weapons against perceived adversaries
Two CPS reports filed against the child's father, both adjudicated Unfounded: one for alleged physical abuse (bruises in an "unusual" but not abuse-conclusive location); one for alleged sexual touching during diapering, which the CPS investigator concluded was a misinterpretation of routine potty-training care, with the child not appearing traumatized and not confirming the allegations. An anonymous fax with a San Ramon area code sent to the father's prospective Bay Area employer (San Jose State ROTC) alleging he was in contempt of court and an unworthy job candidate; this cost him the position and the geographic proximity to the child it would have provided. A TESSA shelter contact for alleged domestic violence. None of these is independently corroborated as substantively true; all of them are documented as having been deployed.

### D. Enmeshment with the child to the child's developmental detriment
This is Gutierrez's clearest forensic finding. The mother-child relationship is described as "enmeshed and exclusive"; the child's "psychological well-being and social functioning are undermined by her parenting"; the child shows "anxious attachment" to her mother characterized by "extreme clingy, whining irritability" alternating with "demanding focus on her, expressed through manifest impatience." Direct observation: mother and child "never more than a foot or two apart"; the maternal grandmother was excluded from interaction; no pictures of the father in the home; spoke only Russian with the child even when explicitly asked to speak English; "encouraged the child's withdrawn and suspicious stance"; modeled toward the child "a demeanor infused with a sense of entitlement, hostility, and mistrust." The child's contrasting presentation with her father (independent, sociable, age-appropriate) was striking enough that two daycare providers and Gutierrez herself remarked on it.

### E. Property and financial conflict behavior, predominantly inside the dyad
Within the marriage: drained joint accounts to pay personal bills; threw away the husband's clothes from the laundry; changed the home security system code before he filed for divorce; deleted his photographs (covering five years) from his Sony image-station account; destroyed photo albums and comments associated with the accounts; tore the wedding entry page from a family chronicles book his parents had given them. These behaviors are domestic and relational, not directed at strangers or at the broader environment.

### F. Hostility toward authority and people who do not comply
Knocking loudly on the evaluator's office door despite posted signage and prior request not to knock. Challenging the evaluator's competence in front of the child. Switching daycare providers when one refused to falsify records. Refusing the original couples therapist's bill (the therapist later refused to speak to Gutierrez until the $300 was paid). The pattern is consistent: people who comply with her are kept; people who do not are dropped, punished, or attacked. This is a relational economy.

## 3. Personality structure: what the testing supports

### Wilbourn's formal Axis II (2007)
Histrionic Personality Disorder with Excessive-Compulsive Personality Traits and Narcissistic Personality Traits. No Axis I diagnosis.

This is the diagnosis a licensed clinical psychologist with a full battery (MMPI-2, MCMI-III, 16PF, PAM, mental status, home visit, eight collaterals) reached. It should be treated as the primary anchoring formulation unless the testing data clearly contradicts it.

### What the testing positively shows
Three findings are unusually robust and matter for what kind of pathology this is:

- **Cognitive function is intact and above average.** MCMI-III: "above-average abstract reasoning ability with a capacity for grasping concepts and processing complicated information." She holds a five-year Russian engineering diploma, expanded her credentials in California, and works in engineering. Mental status in 2007: "all cognitive functions appeared intact." This rules out a cognitive-limitation account of her behavior.

- **Ego strength is high.** 16PF: "exceptional ego strength," "firm sense of self," "steady and resilient identity across many different kinds of situations," "not overly susceptible to variations in self-concept and her focus is not easily swayed by changes in mood." This is the opposite of the identity diffusion seen in BPD and the fragmentation seen in psychotic-spectrum disorders. Whatever is going wrong, it is not happening at the level of self-coherence.

- **No reality-testing impairment.** Mental status in 2007: "no obvious delusions or hallucinations present." Neither evaluator describes paranoid attribution of intent to neutral events, conspiratorial thinking about strangers, or magical-thinking organization of life. The hostility she shows is targeted (toward identifiable adversaries: husband, his family, the evaluator, daycare providers who do not comply), not free-floating.

### What the picture rules out, given the testing
- Borderline Personality Disorder is not supported. The 16PF directly contradicts BPD identity diffusion and affective lability. She did show "tendency toward impulsivity and some anger problems under conditions of conflict" on MMPI-2, but at sub-clinical levels and with "good control of her anger at this time."
- Paranoid Personality Disorder is not supported. No paranoid features in MSE or testing; no collateral reports describing paranoid ideation; the hostility is targeted, not pervasive.
- Antisocial Personality Disorder is not supported. No Axis I or II findings consistent with conduct-disorder history, no callous-unemotional features, no documented criminal record, "Pd" elevation on MMPI-2 was within the within-normal-limits clinical profile. Gutierrez's qualifier matters: "dissimilar to her, he does not seem to be manipulative" was said about Marido, framing manipulation as a feature of *her* presentation, but at a Cluster-B-personality level rather than ASPD-grade.

### What the picture supports, beyond Wilbourn's formal Axis II
The Histrionic-Narcissistic-Obsessive-Compulsive cluster Wilbourn coded fits the behavioral data well:

- *Histrionic* features: gregarious, "outgoing and sociable, she has a strong need to be around others"; dramatic affective communication when conflict starts (TESSA contact, dramatic CPS reports, weeping in interviews); use of the child as an attention object; perfectionistic maintenance of an immaculate home as image-management; the tendency to overstate (e.g., "Marido always had to be right," "Marido is a psycho," "men are not worth much in the family").
- *Narcissistic* features: idealized self-presentation, "tended to emphasize and embellish her virtues"; sense of being outside the rules; entitlement; demand that others modify their behavior to accommodate her preferences (Russian-language testing, late arrivals, knocking on the closed-door); inability to view the father's role as important.
- *Obsessive-Compulsive* features: perfectionism, overconcern with appearances, immaculate home, controlled façade, "tendency toward perfectionism and an overconcern with appearances may become evident."

## 4. Core psychological drivers

### The organizing operating principle
Beneath the surface behaviors, the operative principle appears to be: **the world contains people who confirm my idealized self-image and people who do not, and the latter must be either bent into compliance or removed**. This is not consciously held as a belief; it is enacted. People who comply (her mother, her circle of Russian-American friends, the daycare provider who praises her) are kept and warmly held. People who do not (the husband who tries to leave, the daycare provider who refuses to falsify records, the evaluator who insists on English, the prior couples therapist who labels her "rigid and inflexible") are first pressured, then attacked, then dropped.

### Defense mechanisms
- **Externalization of blame**: Pre-marriage she was demonstrably agentic (left Russia, traveled, attended language school, switched continents, took an engineering job, expanded credentials). In-marriage she narrates everything as something that happened *to* her (move to Colorado happening "too soon," marriage "too soon," pregnancy "too soon," divorce something Marido did to her). Gutierrez named this incongruity directly: "her presentation of herself in these past life changing events as passive seems somewhat incongruous." The narrative shift is the mechanism. Agency is owned when it is consistent with the idealized self; disowned when it would imply responsibility for outcomes that contradict the idealized self.
- **Splitting**: People are categorized as wholly good (mother, daycare provider Waroshevskaya, friends who agree with her) or wholly bad (Marido, his family, Worthley, Wyckoff). Marido's family is "uncultured." Marido is a "psycho." She does not occupy ambivalent positions toward people in her life.
- **Idealization-and-image-management**: Self as bright, well-educated, well-traveled, well-cultured, perfect mother, victim of abuse. These representations are protected from contrary evidence by the defensive structure documented in her testing.
- **Somatization**: Wilbourn explicitly notes the likely pattern: "She may have a history of persistent tensions that are exhibited in functional somatic disorders such as headaches and gastrointestinal difficulties." She takes prescription medication for digestive issues; developed thyroid problems during pregnancy; "visited doctors often."

### What she appears to fear
- **Loss of moral high ground in conflict.** Her behavioral repertoire is built around being seen as the wronged party. Threats to that position activate disproportionate countermeasures.
- **Loss of immigration status.** Multiple collaterals (Tamera Engler, Rebecca Scott, Vanessa Graf) flagged that she would take Anastasia to Russia if denied a green card. The marriage itself partly served this function ("needed to be working or be married").
- **Loss of the child as the central anchor of her identity.** The enmeshment with the child is structural, not incidental. The child is described by Gutierrez as positioned to address the mother's emotional needs rather than her own.

## 5. Marriage and parenting dynamics

### The marriage timeline asymmetry
The marriage timeline contains a pattern that is itself diagnostically informative. She complained extensively to TESSA, to friends, to her mother, to two evaluators, and through two CPS reports. Yet she did not file for divorce. Marido filed in November 2005; she "begged for reconciliation" Christmas Eve and pulled him back; he withdrew the filing end of January 2006. She left for California in April 2006 but still did not file. Marido refiled in May 2006. She never filed in this marriage at all.

A genuinely battered or controlled spouse, given an exit, normally takes it. She did not take it. The complaints functioned as currency *inside* the marriage, not as exit signals. They served to maintain the moral high ground, to recruit external sympathy and institutional resources, and to keep the partner on the defensive. When the partner attempted to actually exit, the currency was put aside and reconciliation pursued. Two practical considerations shape the timing: her green card was tied to the marriage during this window, and the daughter served as both reason to stay and lever to extract resources.

### The parenting picture
The picture is genuinely mixed and the evidence has to be held together to make sense.

What she does well: the home is consistently immaculate; the child is bright, bilingual, and securely fed and clothed; the child shows clear love and attachment to her; she structures cultural enrichment (museums, aquarium, books, ballet); friends and one daycare provider describe her as a "perfect mom." This is the surface-level competence both evaluators acknowledge.

What she does poorly: the relationship is enmeshed and exclusionary; the child's age-appropriate independence is discouraged rather than supported; the father's presence in the child's life is actively undermined through coached statements, language barriers, withholding, and false allegations; the maternal grandmother's interaction with the child is also curtailed. The child's behavior contrasts so sharply between the two parents (clingy, regressed, mute around evaluator with mother; independent, conversational, "I'm happy today!" with father) that the maternal environment is producing developmental cost the paternal environment is not.

The two pictures are not contradictory. She is competent at the *production* aspects of parenting (food, home, education, clothes, cultural enrichment) and impaired at the *separation* aspects (allowing the child to develop a self separate from her, allowing other adults a meaningful role in the child's life, allowing the father to be a father). The impairment is not a failure of effort or attention; it is structural to her relational style.

### Forensic finding worth taking seriously
Wilbourn: "capable of engaging in parental alienation, and then calling it something else." Gutierrez 2009 confirms the pattern through direct observation, multiple collaterals, and the contrast in the child's two presentations. This is not speculation. It is a finding both evaluators reached.

## 6. Cognitive profile

The MCMI-III in 2007 explicitly documents above-average abstract reasoning ability and a capacity for grasping concepts and processing complicated information. She holds a Russian engineering diploma. She runs household and immigration administration competently. She is articulate in two languages. She cannot reasonably be characterized as cognitively limited or concrete-bound.

What the evaluations do support is **defensive cognitive narrowing under threat**: she will not engage abstract dialogue about topics that threaten her self-image, her belief systems, or her sense of being the wronged party. Vanessa Graf described it as "rigid belief in her own plan." This is *motivational* rigidity, not capacity-based.

She also shows an unusual pattern that Wilbourn flagged: high abstract reasoning capability paired with limited self-reflection. The MCMI-III narrative hints at this: "It is somewhat unusual to find someone this intelligence has been sealed away from emotional discourse such as she... she may lack confidence in the quality of her ideas. When she does express herself, her discomfort with the assertive role can result in her not being heard as she thinks." The intellectual capacity is present and the emotional-discursive capacity is sealed off. The defensiveness is not a substitute for thinking; it is a wall around it.

## 7. Attachment and developmental context

The available data is limited and what is there is filtered through her own self-presentation, which is image-managed. Treat the developmental section as the most uncertain part of this analysis.

What the documents support:

- **Father absence at age 3-4**, framed by her in culturally normalized terms ("it wasn't that uncommon that dads would just take off, and kids would grow up with just their moms"). The biological father remained alive in Russia, visited occasionally, but was not a sustained presence. This is the only documented loss of attachment figure.
- **Mother as the dominant adult attachment**, sustained into adulthood. Described as "wonderful," involved, structured Russian-traditional childhood; remained the dominant figure in her life through two countries and one marriage; visited the US repeatedly and provided childcare for Anastasia. Her adult relational pattern continues to position her own mother as the central figure (the maternal grandmother attended the home visit, was urged by her to speak through her, and is the only adult she lets near the child without restriction).
- **No documented stepfather, abuse, neglect, or trauma.** She describes her childhood in idealized terms ("blissful," "great life"). The evaluators do not contradict this from collateral evidence, though the idealized framing is itself a defensive feature.
- **A culturally transmitted belief** that "in Russia men are not worth much in the family." Disclosed in therapy. Both evaluators flagged this as prognostic for paternal estrangement.

What can be said tentatively, given those facts:
The configuration looks like an **anxious-preoccupied attachment to female caregiving figures (mother, daughter) paired with a dismissive-avoidant stance toward male partners**, rationalized through the cultural belief that men are dispensable. This is not disorganized attachment in the classical sense, and there is no positive evidence of caregiver-as-source-of-fear (which would be needed to call it disorganized). The maternal bond was protective and remains so. The paternal absence has been culturally absorbed rather than personally grieved.

This developmental picture is consistent with the adult Histrionic-Narcissistic presentation: a child who grew up the only daughter of an admiring mother in a setting with no countervailing male voice, who developed the felt sense of being central and admirable, and who carried that felt sense into adulthood without ever encountering a relational structure that required her to revise it.

## 8. The cultural frame

The disclosure to Vanessa Graf, that "in Russia the belief is that men are not worth much in the family," is a single sentence but it does load-bearing work in the case file. It explains, without idiosyncrasy, why she:

- Treats Marido's parental role as superfluous (Wilbourn's finding)
- Considers her daughter's emotional needs transferable "to another adult male" (Wilbourn)
- Describes Marido's family as "uncultured" and herself as not requiring their input
- Excludes the father from the child's home environment (no pictures, no language access)
- Maintains the maternal grandmother as the only legitimate adult co-parent

The cultural belief does not generate the pathology. Many people from the same cultural setting do not behave this way. But it provides a ready-made rationalization for behaviors the personality structure was already inclined toward. The HPD-NPD core wants the partner-as-audience without partner-as-co-equal; the cultural belief gives that wanting a respectable language.

## 9. The self-narrative incongruity

The most useful single observation either evaluator made about her, in terms of formulation, is Gutierrez's: her "presentation of herself in these past life changing events as passive seems somewhat incongruous."

The actual pre-marriage she: left Russia at age 24 alone, attended a language school in New York, settled in the Bay Area, worked her way into an engineering job, expanded her credentials at a community college, navigated a work visa.

The narrated marriage she: things happened too fast; she had to leave a job she loved; she had to move to Colorado; she got pregnant before she was ready; she got married when she would have preferred to wait; she got divorced because Marido did things to her.

The shift between these two voices is one of the most diagnostically clean findings in the file. It tells you that her relationship to her own agency is selective: agency is owned where it confirms the idealized self and disowned where it would imply responsibility for outcomes that contradict the idealized self. This is the single feature most likely to make insight-oriented therapy difficult, because a patient cannot examine choices she does not narrate herself as having made.

## 10. Prognosis (within the 2007-2009 horizon)

Wilbourn's prognosis sentence in 2007 is the most carefully chosen line in his report and worth quoting one more time:

> "Individuals with this level of defensiveness do not view themselves as being in need of behavior change. They typically do not seek psychological treatment on their own and are reluctant to get involved if they are pushed into therapy."

Two years later Gutierrez sees the same picture, and her recommendations include the same therapy-as-prerequisite logic, with limited evidence she expects compliance.

What is likely to happen, based on these two evaluations alone:

- **No spontaneous treatment-seeking.** The defensive structure prevents the self-perception that would motivate it.
- **Continued use of legal and institutional channels** to manage adversaries, with the CPS-report pattern as the most concerning template (because false sexual abuse allegations are the most damaging form available and she has used it at least once).
- **Continued enmeshment with the child**, with the developmental cost predicted by Gutierrez (suspiciousness, interpersonal hostility toward others, peer-relationship difficulty, kindergarten functioning at risk).
- **Probable consolidation rather than improvement of the pattern with age**, absent a forcing event.
- **Forecasting beyond ~5 years from the second evaluation is unsupported by the data in these documents alone.**

## 11. Treatment considerations

The treatments that target this presentation, in approximate order of fit:

- **Mentalization-Based Treatment (MBT).** Targets exactly the limited insight, the splitting, the disowned agency, and the inability to think about her own and others' inner states under conflict. The therapeutic relationship itself becomes the laboratory. Requires sustained engagement.
- **Schema therapy.** Targets the rigid self-concept ("I am the wronged party") and the cultural-and-family-of-origin schemas (men are dispensable, mothers and daughters are inseparable, rules are for others). Has the advantage of working with material she can speak about (her mother, her childhood, her family) rather than material she cannot (her own dysregulation).
- **Couples or co-parenting therapy with a clinician familiar with high-conflict divorce and personality dynamics.** Both evaluators recommended this. The catch is that she must already be in individual treatment for the couples format to do useful work.
- **Cognitive-behavioral approaches focused on parenting skills.** Lower fit given that the impairment is relational rather than skill-based, but might serve as an entry point given her preference for concrete, structured activity.

The treatment that is *not* indicated by either of these documents alone:

- DBT, in its classical form, is designed for affective dysregulation and impulsive self-damaging behavior. The 2007-2009 picture does not show that level of dysregulation. DBT skills (mindfulness, distress tolerance, interpersonal effectiveness) might still be useful but the full DBT framework is overkill for this presentation.
- Pharmacotherapy is not indicated by the 2007-2009 picture. There is no Axis I disorder in either evaluation. No mood disorder, no anxiety disorder severe enough to medicate, no psychotic features. Functional somatic features are best addressed in the therapy space rather than pharmacologically.

The single largest barrier to any of these working is the same one Wilbourn named: she would need to seek help and stay in it long enough for it to work, and the defensive structure pushes against both.

## 12. What is striking about this picture as a whole

A few things stand out when the case is considered on its own terms.

**The pathology is articulate.** This is not a chaotic, incoherent presentation. It is structured, coherent, internally consistent, and repeatable. She does the same things across husbands, daycare providers, friends, employers, evaluators, courts. The pattern is stable enough to be diagnosed and named.

**The cognitive function is high.** This is not a person who cannot understand institutions, contracts, immigration law, custody arrangements, or her own behavior. She can. She chooses how she presents what she understands.

**The damage is targeted, not diffuse.** Strangers are not at risk. Random adversaries are not at risk. The people at risk are people in primary relational positions: husband, child, in-laws, providers who become stand-ins for caregiving relationships. The pathology is built around the relational dyad, not around the world at large.

**The institutional channels she uses are legitimate channels.** TESSA, CPS, family court, divorce filings, custody evaluations, medical providers. She is not breaking the law to get her ends met. She is using legitimate processes in ways the processes do not anticipate. This makes her difficult to constrain through the usual institutional means, because each individual use is hard to call abuse-of-process even when the pattern is.

**The child is the most exposed party.** Husbands can leave. Friends can leave. Providers can be replaced. The child cannot. The 2009 evaluator's central concern is that the child's developmental trajectory is being shaped by an enmeshed maternal environment that is not being corrected by any countervailing influence. That is the most important single finding in the file, and it is the one that most clearly survives all the other analytical questions.

## 13. Caveats

- This analysis is built on two evaluations. It is not the same thing as a clinical evaluation by a clinician who has met her.
- The diagnostic anchor (HPD with Narcissistic and Obsessive-Compulsive traits, no Axis I) is from 2007. People can change. The 2009 picture is consistent, but two years is two years.
- The evaluations are themselves filtered through the case context. Both were forensic, both were court-ordered, and both subjects had instrumental reasons to manage their presentation. Wilbourn explicitly notes the MMPI-2 was invalidated by defensiveness; the 16PF profile cautioned that some positive inferences "may have been based on a possible effort to look good."
- The collateral interviews are themselves partial. Her side's collaterals describe her positively; his side's describe her negatively. The objective collaterals (the daycare providers Worthley and Wyckoff, the daycare director's translator Buchanan, both CPS investigators) trend toward concern, but no collateral is unsituated.
- Nothing in this analysis bears on what may have happened after September 2009.
