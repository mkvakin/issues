**Plain-language interlude on the next two paragraphs.** A few terms recur that are worth defining once:

- **Somatic** means "of the body." A "somatic stress response" is the body part of stress: heart rate up, gut clenched, chest tight, breath shortened. It is the visible/feelable physiology of activation.
- **Autonomic** refers to the autonomic nervous system, which runs the involuntary stuff (heart, gut, sweat, pupil size). It has a "fight/flight" branch (sympathetic) that fires under threat, and a "rest/digest" branch (parasympathetic) that settles you afterward. When you read "the body fires the autonomic response," that is the heart-and-gut alarm switching on without your having to decide.
- **Affect** is the felt-and-shown emotion (anger, sadness, fear, joy). "Affective experience" is what it feels like inside; "affective expression" is what shows on the outside.
- **Alexithymia** literally means "no words for emotions." In practice it describes people whose body has the emotion (heart racing, jaw tight) but whose conscious mind cannot name what they are feeling. It exists on a spectrum and can be domain-specific: someone may be alexithymic for *anger* but perfectly fluent in noticing curiosity, fondness, sadness.

**The key finding from the Q&A**: the body fires the autonomic stress response in confrontation-shaped moments (chest tightness, gut symptoms), but the conscious feeling of *anger* never arrives. The body knows; the mind has been trained not to know.

The first pass of this analysis labeled that pattern "alexithymia narrowed to the anger channel." With the third addendum (eye contact, smile, name use, avoidance of self-marking, calling parents "he/she"), that label is now too narrow. The pattern is better described as **inhibition of direct expressed affect across the board, with anger as the most severely closed sub-channel**. See Section 3.7 for the full unpacking. For Section 2's purposes, the corrected summary is:

This reframes the problem. He is not a chronically anxious man who needs his anxiety reduced. He is a calm, agreeable, introverted man whose **direct face-to-face emotional contact is dampened on the expression side** — anger is the most severely closed, but warmth-shown, recognition-claimed, and being-seen are also dialed down. His body keeps firing the underlying alarm in the right moments. The mismatch between the body (firing) and the visible interpersonal output (muted) is the central somatic-emotional finding.

## 3. The Specific Lesion: Anger, Not Fear (And How I Got There)

This section is worth slowing down on, because the move from "you have a fear problem" to "you have an anger-and-confrontation-output problem" is the central reframe of v2, and the chain of reasoning is not obvious. Here is the chain, step by step.

### Step 1. The original self-report named the problem as "fear," but described it strangely.

The original `him.md` said *fear-driven*. But notice what was missing: there was no description of the *experience* of fear. No racing thoughts about disasters. No "what if it goes wrong" loops. No catastrophizing. No phobias. He explicitly said the fear is not felt acutely in the moment; it is "buried in the subconscious" and runs decisions from underneath. That description, taken literally, is unusual. Most fear-driven people have plenty of conscious fear. They are flooded with it. He is not flooded with fear. He is flooded with **nothing** in the moments where anger should be.

### Step 2. The Big Five and screening tools confirmed the absence of generic anxiety.

GAD-7 came back almost entirely negative: no chronic worry, no inability to relax, no irritability, no foreboding. Trait Neuroticism low. PHQ-9 also negative. So whatever the issue is, it is not "anxiety" in any of the standard senses. That ruled out the most common explanation for "fear runs my life" presentations.

### Step 3. The body's signal is present; the felt emotion is absent.

Q44 was the pivot question: *"When you are angry now, where in your body do you feel it?"* The answer was: *"I don't feel anger. I feel overstressed. Chest tightness."* That is the diagnostic sentence. The body is firing (chest tightness is the autonomic response), but the affect being reported is not anger. It is "overstressed." The body has the emotion. The mind has substituted a vague, non-specific label that does not point toward any action.

This is the fingerprint of **alexithymia for anger**: present body, absent name, absent action.

### Step 4. The behavioral evidence converges.

Once that fingerprint is in view, a long list of items snaps into one shape:

- Stranger cuts in line: nothing. (Q46)
- Colleague takes credit: nothing. (Q47)
- Confrontation with zero consequences: still defaults to "cultured behavior." (Q49)
- Last visible anger: doesn't remember. Even during separation: reserved. (Q43)
- Has not broken anything, shouted, or used force as an adult. (Q48)
- Schema "I let others have their way because I fear consequences" → somewhat true. (Q56)
- "Replacement with zero fear" answer: the copy would have *better communication openness, less narrowness*. (Q111). The implied missing thing is interpersonal-communication confidence, not generic courage.
- Self-Witness: avoids communication; sometimes doesn't listen; says only what he wants to say. (Q112). The social channel is partially read-only.
- Cost of dropping "civilized" for one month: "Breakup with people." (Q129). This is the operating belief that locks the rule in place.

Each item alone could be many things. Together they form a shape: *the channel that produces anger-as-a-felt-emotion and confrontation-as-an-action is closed.* What pours through it on the body side (chest tightness) and what comes out on the action side ("cultured behavior") are clear; the middle, where the felt anger and the assertive output should live, is empty.

### Step 5. The contrast with bold actions confirms the diagnosis is local, not global.

If this were "fear runs his life broadly," he would not have migrated countries on his own initiative, he would not have ended his first marriage on a deadline ultimatum, he would not have steered a sailboat through a storm with sharp decisions. He did all three. So whatever the issue is, it is **not generic risk-aversion or generic courage deficit**. It is specifically activated in one shape of situation: high-emotional-stakes interpersonal confrontation, especially where continued attachment is in play.

### Step 6. The age-13 transition + family norm + childhood timidity gives the historical mechanism (revised).

The original v2 traced the closure to a specific event-cluster at age 13–14 (robbery, recognition that fights have physical consequences) plus a family norm ("we don't project feelings"). The addendum changes the timeline: the user reports timidity from early childhood (later to swim, later to bike than sister; afraid of new settings like a hairdresser or a photographer's studio). So the chain is now:

- **Layer 1 — Temperament (from birth)**: a cautious, slow-to-warm-to-novelty constitution. In developmental psychology this is called **behavioral inhibition** (see Section 3.5 below).
- **Layer 2 — Family environment (ages 0–13)**: a soft norm against expressing strong feelings, especially anger. Conflict-avoidant father; a mother who was warm but accommodating. The temperament was validated and reinforced rather than challenged.
- **Layer 3 — Age-13/14 event-cluster**: the robbery and physical-consequences realization specifically locked the *anger / confrontation* channel, on top of an already cautious substrate. This is the moment the rule "if I get angry or confront, something physical or social-relational may break" became a fixed routing rule.
- **Layer 4 — Adult elaboration (ages 14–60)**: the routing rule continued to operate. The autonomic system kept firing in confrontation-shaped moments. The output kept being "cultured behavior." The pattern got more sophisticated (Soviet-intelligentsia self-presentation, professional norms, Stoic philosophy as post-hoc validation) but the underlying rule did not change.

### Read together, the model is:

The age-13–14 event-cluster locked an anger-and-confrontation routing rule on top of an already cautious temperament and an already conflict-avoidant family climate. Decades later, the autonomic alarm still fires in the right moments (chest tightness when something matters), but the felt anger and the assertive output never arrive at the surface. He labels the body sensation "overstressed" because that is the only label the channel still allows. **The work is to reopen the channel so the body's signal can finally arrive at the conscious surface, where it can be felt, named, and converted into action.**

This is much more specific than "fear runs his life." It is also much more treatable. **Anger-recovery work** exists as a discrete therapeutic target with established methods.

## 3.5 The Childhood-Timidity Layer (Temperament)

The addendum reports a pattern that v2's first pass missed: timidity *before* age 13. Learning to swim later than the older sister because of fear. Learning to bicycle later for the same reason. Avoiding the hairdresser and the professional-photo studio because the new setting was scary. These are not small details. They change the model.

### What this looks like in developmental psychology

Jerome Kagan, working at Harvard from the 1980s onward, identified a temperamental pattern he called **behavioral inhibition (BI)**. It shows up in roughly 15-20% of children and is observable as early as 4 months old. Behaviorally inhibited children share a cluster of features:

- Slow to warm up to new settings, new people, new objects.
- Higher baseline autonomic reactivity: heart rate up faster, pupils dilate more, cortisol higher in response to novelty.
- More cautious about physical risk.
- Observe before approaching; rarely the first child into the pool.
- Often prefer one or two close attachments to large group play.

BI is partly genetic (about 40-50% heritable, with measurable amygdala differences in fMRI studies of inhibited adults) and partly shaped by parenting and exposure. It is **not pathology**. It is a normal variation in temperament. Most BI children grow into reserved, thoughtful, careful adults; about 15% of BI children develop full-blown anxiety disorders later, and most do not.

### Why this matters for the analysis

The age-13 event-cluster (robbery, physical-consequences realization) does not need to **create** the cautious pattern from nothing. It only needs to **lock** an existing temperamental disposition into a specific routing rule for a specific channel (anger and confrontation). Many behaviorally inhibited children move through adolescence without their anger channel closing; this man's combination of BI substrate + conflict-avoidant family + age-13 robbery produced a more locked-down outcome than the temperament alone would have.

This changes three things:

1. **Some of the cautiousness is not a problem to be solved**. It is who he is, and trying to become bold-by-default would be both miserable and unnecessary. The Silicon-Valley migration, the storm-sailing, the ultimatum-divorce show that the temperament does not actually prevent decisive action. It just makes decisive action take longer to mobilize, and means decisive action will be carefully chosen rather than impulsive.

2. **The anger channel is a more isolated lesion than v2 first suggested**. Cautious temperament + closed anger channel are different things. The cautious temperament colors his daily style (introvert, observer, slow reader, deep absorption, drained by parties). The closed anger channel is a specific functional block in the confrontation domain. Therapy targets the second; the first does not need to be "fixed."

3. **The "fear" the original `him.md` named is partly the temperament being misread as a problem.** A behaviorally inhibited adult living in a culture (Silicon Valley, US tech) that treats extroversion and bold-confrontation as default norms will often experience his own temperament as a deficit. It is not. It is a different operating system that requires different strategies (deep small circle rather than broad networking; long preparation before bold moves rather than improvisation; quiet decisive action rather than loud charisma).

### Practical implication

Read **Susan Cain's *Quiet*** as the cultural reframe of introversion and Cain's downstream work *Bittersweet* if the existential thread resonates. Stop treating "I am cautious by nature" as a defect to overcome and start treating it as a constraint to design around. The anger-channel work in Section 7 is not negated by this; it is sharpened. The work is to reopen one specific closed channel, not to become a different person.

## 3.6 The Second Driver: Fear of Being Alone

The addendum names another driver that v2's first pass folded inside "fear of confrontation" and so misread. The driver is **fear of being alone**. In the user's own words: it explains the choice of women despite seeing problems from the start, the attachment to Rarus Health, and the recognition that he cannot rely on his own skills to build a "pipeline of contacts." This is a separate construct from confrontation-avoidance, and it is load-bearing for several patterns the first pass left as loose threads.

### What this is, in plain terms

Fear of being alone is not the same as **attachment anxiety**. Attachment anxiety (the kind measured by ECR-R) is the worry that *specific people you love* will leave you, abandon you, or stop caring. He scored low on that. Fear of being alone is more general: it is the dread of the *state* of having no anchor, no project to belong to, no person who knows him, no group that requires him. It can coexist with secure attachment to specific people. It can also coexist with introversion (he doesn't need a lot of people; he needs *some*, reliably). And it can be present without conscious recognition, expressed only through the choices it shapes.

In Young's schema therapy vocabulary, this maps onto a milder version of two schemas blended together:

- **Social Isolation / Alienation**: the sense that one is fundamentally outside, different, not part of a group.
- **Dependence / Incompetence (interpersonal slice only)**: the belief that one cannot independently build and sustain the social structure one needs, and so must hold tightly to whatever structure exists.

He does not meet criteria for either schema in full form (he is independently capable in many domains, including making friends through shared work). But there is a partial form active in the social domain specifically, expressed as a preference for *holding on* rather than *generating new*.

### How this driver shows up in the data

Once you look for it, the pattern is consistent:

- **The relationship choices**. v2's first pass said he "stayed past the point of clarity in three relationships." The standard reading was confrontation avoidance. The fuller reading is: confrontation avoidance + aloneness avoidance, **stacked**. To leave a bad relationship requires both (a) doing the hard conversation and (b) tolerating the period of being alone afterward. Even if (a) were free, (b) would still be blocking. He stated this himself: each relationship ended only when an alternative existed, was forced by ultimatum, or was ended by the partner. The exit pattern is consistent with aloneness being the more painful side of the trade.

- **The choice of partners despite early-visible problems**. This is the addendum's own self-insight. If aloneness is the deepest aversive, then "available and willing" beats "right." The drinker first wife was loyal. The second was manipulative but present. The third had visible anger problems but was a binding partner who would not leave. Each was, from the aloneness frame, a guarantee of non-aloneness. The cost (misery) was tolerated because the alternative (solitude) was worse.

- **The Rarus Health attachment**. He named Rarus as the project that "feels alive" (Q105). That is real, and the project is meaningful in its own right. But the addendum surfaces the structural function: Rarus also provides a ready-made circle of colleagues, a daily reason to interact, a shared identity, and a future-vector ("possible exit; meaningful work"). These are exactly the things a man with a closed anger channel and an aloneness fear most needs. The risk is **anchor concentration**: if the project does not succeed, multiple psychological needs collapse simultaneously, not just the financial one.

- **"Cannot build a pipeline of contacts"**. He says this explicitly. The phrase is a software engineer's way of saying "I cannot reliably manufacture new relationships when I need them." That is a real self-knowledge moment. It is partly the BI temperament (initiating new contact is energetically expensive), partly the closed confrontation channel (because new relationships sometimes require it), and partly the absence of practice. It is not a character flaw. It is a structural consequence of two of the patterns this analysis already names. But it sets up the aloneness fear: if you cannot make new connections at will, then the connections you have become irreplaceable, and losing them feels existentially threatening rather than just sad.

- **One close friend (Q89)**. v2 already flagged this. The addendum makes the flag more urgent. A man with one close friend who *also* fears aloneness *and* whose ability to make new connections is structurally limited has a fragile social ledger. Any disruption to that one friendship would be a real crisis.

- **Stable identity, but unsettled when stripped of roles (Q88)**. He answered that imagining himself "without his roles" was unsettling. The standard reading is identity diffusion. The fuller reading: roles are not just identity, they are also relational scaffolding. Stripped of "engineer, husband, father, immigrant," there are no relationships left, because his relationships are largely role-mediated. The unsettled feeling is partly aloneness preview.

- **The eulogy shame line (Q113)**: "didn't reach anything of significance because he was always in fear to step over himself." The unspoken half is "and I lived a life organized to keep people around me even when stepping over myself would have meant losing some of them." Aloneness is what stepping-over-yourself sometimes costs. He is naming the cost without yet naming what made the cost feel unbearable.

### How the two drivers interact

The two drivers do not just coexist. They reinforce each other in a specific way:

- The closed anger channel makes confrontation impossible.
- Confrontation in close relationships is sometimes how relationships *survive* (you say the truth, the relationship is stronger for it) and sometimes how they *end* (you say the truth, the relationship breaks). Either way, it requires confronting.
- The aloneness fear makes endings intolerable.
- So the rule becomes: *never confront, because the confrontation might end the relationship, and an ended relationship moves me toward the state I most fear.*
- This rule produces both observed patterns: the cultured-behavior default in the moment, and the staying-too-long pattern across years.

The two drivers also explain why the v1 framing of "fear runs his life" felt right at the level of phenomenology even when it failed at the diagnostic level. The phenomenological experience of the two drivers stacking is something that feels exactly like "fear is at the bottom of everything." Because, in a structural sense, it is. The fear is fear-of-aloneness, and it is at the bottom of the avoidance stack. Above it sits the closed anger channel. Above that sits the cultured-behavior surface. The user has been describing the experience of the whole stack as "fear-driven life," and he was not wrong about the experience. He was wrong only about the nature and number of the fears.

### Treatment implications of adding this driver

1. **Aloneness work is a separate therapeutic target**. ACT and schema therapy already do part of this (committed action despite uncomfortable feeling; schema-mode work). But there is a specific addition: **deliberate practice tolerating short, contained periods of aloneness**, with the explicit goal of demonstrating to the system that aloneness is survivable and that the person who comes back from a solo three-day trip is not damaged. This is exposure therapy applied to a state, not to a stimulus.

2. **Build social structure with redundancy, not concentration**. Rarus is one anchor. There should be three to five. A men's group is a second. A reactivated old friendship is a third. A shared physical activity (sailing club, hiking group) is a fourth. The principle: no single relationship or project should be load-bearing for non-aloneness. If it is, the system will not allow it to be challenged or lost, and that constraint will distort decision-making in that domain.

3. **Name the aloneness fear out loud**. He already named "I am afraid" (Q123) as the never-said sentence. The addendum lets us extend it: *"I am afraid of being alone."* Said out loud to a therapist or trusted person, it does specific work: it converts an unconscious driver into a conscious one. Unconscious drivers run decisions silently. Conscious drivers can be argued with.

4. **Do not confuse aloneness work with becoming an extrovert**. He is a real introvert. The work is not to fill his life with people. The work is to make sure that the small number of people and projects that anchor his life are *not held tightly because losing them would be unbearable*, but held warmly because they are good. The grip is the problem; the connection itself is fine.

## 3.7 Face-to-Face Inhibition: A Wider Pattern Than Anger

A third addendum surfaces five behaviors that the first two passes did not have. Read together they change the diagnosis again — not by overturning what is already there, but by widening it. The closed channel is not just anger; it is the whole class of *direct, claimed, face-to-face affective contact*. Anger is the most severely closed sub-channel. Other sub-channels are also dampened.

### The five new data points

1. **Dislike of calling people by name.** Names are claims. To say "Andrey, …" is to assert the relationship in a way that "hey, …" does not. He prefers the lower-claim form.
2. **Difficulty calling parents *папа* and *мама*; uses "he" and "she" instead.** This is the most diagnostic of the five. In Russian, addressing parents as *папа* and *мама* is the warm-default register. The third-person bypass ("он," "она") creates linguistic distance from the most primary attachment figures. This is not about the parents being bad — by his account they were warm and reliable — it is about his comfort with the *direct intimate-naming register* itself.
3. **Aversion to wearing rings, watches, chains.** Body decoration marks the self as a defined object. Rings in particular signify roles (wedding ring), commitments, claims. The aversion is to being marked, to the self being made visibly distinctive or claimable.
4. **Avoidance of eye contact.** Eye contact is the most direct form of mutual attunement. To hold someone's gaze is to be open to being read and to read in return. Avoidance is a withdrawal from this mutual openness.
5. **Shyness about smiling openly into another's face.** The open smile is a direct expression of warmth toward another. It is also vulnerability — exposed teeth, exposed pleasure, exposed wanting. Inhibiting it inhibits expressed warmth in face-to-face moments.

### What these five things share

Each one is, in its own way, an avoidance of *direct, claimed, mutual interpersonal contact in the warm or recognizing register*. Names claim. Eye contact attunes. Smiles offer warmth. Body adornment marks the self as visible and claimable. Calling parents "папа/мама" performs the bond out loud. He pulls back from each of these in small, often invisible ways.

This is the same channel that the anger work targets, but seen from the *expressive* side rather than the *anger-specific* side. The earlier framing — "alexithymia for anger only" — was only about half the picture. The more accurate framing:

- The *receiving* side of emotion works fine. He feels affection, gratitude, warmth, sadness internally. He registers others' emotions. Curiosity, interest, fondness, sadness all read normally on the inside.
- The *expressing* side is dampened in the face-to-face register. Particularly: anger (most severe), warmth-shown, recognition-claimed, being-seen-positively. He can express these in writing, at distance, through action. He cannot easily express them in the moment, in person, looking the other in the eye.
- The *somatic* side fires in both directions: chest tightness in confrontation moments, but probably also held-back smiles and averted gazes in warm moments. The body has the affect; the face does not show it; the channel is one-way.

### Diagnostic vocabulary that fits

In schema-therapy terms, the **Emotional Inhibition schema** is broader than v2's first pass said. It is not "severe in the anger domain, mild elsewhere." It is **moderate-to-severe across the entire face-to-face expressive domain**, with the anger sub-channel being the most severely closed.

In personality-spectrum terms, this is consistent with **mild schizoid-spectrum traits**. Not Schizoid Personality Disorder, which is a much more severe and pervasive pattern of preference for solitude over intimacy, with profound affective constriction. He has secure attachment, three serious relationships, capacity for warmth, professional engagement with people, deep absorption in shared work. He is not schizoid. But he sits in the part of the personality space where the schizoid spectrum lives — the territory of valuing privacy of self, preferring connection at a slight remove, being uncomfortable with the demanding directness of fully-present intimacy. This is a flavor, not a disorder.

In neurodevelopmental terms, two of the items (eye-contact avoidance, dislike of being looked at) are weakly suggestive of autism-spectrum-adjacent features. Most introverts have some eye-contact discomfort and most non-autistic people can dislike being the focus of visual attention; the pattern alone does not push toward an ASD diagnosis. But it is worth naming in the differential and worth considering if ever there is reason to investigate further. Most likely it is not ASD; it is the BI-temperament + emotional-inhibition combination expressing itself in the face-to-face channel.

### How this interacts with the two drivers

The two drivers in v2 — closed anger channel and fear of being alone — now have to be read alongside this third pattern.

- **The aloneness paradox sharpens.** He fears the state of being alone, yet he avoids the very behaviors (calling people by name, holding gaze, smiling openly, marking himself as theirs through ring/watch/chain) that build and signal the bonds that prevent aloneness. The wanted condition (presence of others) is in tension with the avoided actions (the moves that produce that presence). This is classic schizoid-spectrum tension: wants connection, avoids the cost of full intimate contact. The way the system has historically resolved this is by binding tightly to a small number of people who do not require much expressive warmth from him in the face-to-face register, and accepting that he will pay for that in other ways (their dysregulation, their possessiveness, etc.). His third partner specifically did not require warm face-to-face from him. She required loyalty and presence and tolerance. Those were within the channel he could supply.
- **The anger-channel work changes shape.** Anger-recovery alone, narrowly conceived, will not resolve the wider pattern. The work has to widen to include practice in the *whole* expressive face-to-face channel, not just the anger sub-channel. Practical implications below.
- **The cultural overlay is real and partially benign.** Soviet-intelligentsia restraint, the family "we don't project feelings on others" norm, and ordinary Russian-cultural lower-default-warmth-expression all overlap with this pattern. Some of what looks like inhibition is just culturally calibrated reserve. Distinguishing what is cultural-calibration (leave alone) from what is functional-deficit (work on) is part of the therapeutic work itself.

### How to weight the new pattern relative to v1's "fear runs my life"

This is the v3 picture. The original "fear-driven life" framing pointed at the right phenomenology and at the wrong mechanism. The corrected mechanism is now three-layered:

1. **Temperament**: behaviorally inhibited from birth.
2. **Two specific drivers**: closed anger channel + fear of aloneness.
3. **Wider expressive inhibition**: the whole face-to-face affective channel is dampened on the expression side, with anger being the most severe.

Each layer has different treatment implications and different leverage points. Together they explain almost every behavior in the original `him.md` and the addenda.

### Practice implications

These are concrete, low-stakes daily practices that target the wider channel, separate from the anger-specific work in Section 7:

- **Use one person's name out loud per day**, in conversation. Doesn't matter whose. The point is to repeat the act of direct claimed address until it stops feeling foreign.
- **Try saying *папа* or *мама* out loud once**, in a low-stakes moment, even if it feels awkward. Notice what comes up. The discomfort itself is the data; the work is to allow the address rather than route around it.
- **Hold gaze for two seconds longer than feels natural**, with one person each day. Two seconds. Not a stare. Just a slightly-extended moment of mutual contact. The system will calibrate.

## 7. Treatment Plan, Updated

The v1 plan was directionally right but misallocated. v2 reweights.

### Tier 1 (highest expected value)

**Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) — unchanged in priority.**

(In plain terms: **ACT** is a third-wave cognitive-behavioral therapy whose core idea is that the goal is not to eliminate uncomfortable feelings but to act in line with your values *while* having them. It teaches you to notice difficult thoughts and feelings as passing experiences rather than commands, and to commit to action that matches what you actually care about. The two key skills are *defusion*, which is the ability to see a thought as a thought rather than as the truth, and *committed action*, which is doing the values-aligned thing even when the feeling argues against it.)

Still the best fit. The user already shows the ACT skill of acting under acute fear (sailing in a storm, Q128 = "fully alive"). The work is to extend that skill from physical-emergency contexts to interpersonal-emergency contexts. The leverage point: he has *demonstrated* he can act under fear when the fear is physical and the context is concrete. He has not yet learned that the same skill transfers to the moment of saying a hard sentence to his wife.

**Schema Therapy — narrowed target.**

(In plain terms: **schema therapy** combines pieces of CBT, attachment theory, and gestalt techniques. Its central idea is that long-standing patterns of belief and feeling (schemas) get installed in childhood and run automatically in adulthood, often without our noticing. The therapy uses experiential techniques like *imagery work* — guided revisiting of a childhood scene with adult perspective — and *chair work* — moving between chairs to externalize and dialogue with different parts of the self — to make schemas visible and modifiable.)

Focused on **Emotional Inhibition** as the primary schema, **Subjugation** as secondary, and **Aloneness-Anxiety / Social Isolation** as the new tertiary target identified in the addendum. Specific imagery work for the age-13–14 event-cluster and chair work for the conflict-avoidant family voice. Resources: *Reinventing Your Life* (Young & Klosko).

**Somatic / Anger-Recovery Work — newly elevated.**

This is the *new* tier-1 addition based on the Q&A. The anger channel is closed at the affective level but firing at the somatic level. The work has two faces:

- **Somatic Experiencing** (Peter Levine) or **Hakomi** approaches. *Somatic Experiencing* is a body-oriented therapy that tracks where activation lives in the body (chest tightness, jaw clench, gut tension), titrates contact with that activation in small doses, and lets the autonomic nervous system complete the response cycles it never finished. The principle is that stuck activation is what doesn't have room to move; given room, it discharges. *Hakomi* is a related body-mindfulness approach with a more relational frame.
- **Anger-recovery groups / men's work**: e.g., Mankind Project's New Warrior Training (a structured weekend) or therapist-facilitated men's groups. The point is not "anger management" (he doesn't have an excess problem); it is **anger retrieval** (he has a deficit problem). Many people who do this work describe the experience of "rediscovering" an emotion they had effectively shelved. The men's-group format is also a partial solution to the aloneness driver: structured masculine peer relationships are an anchor type the user does not currently have.
- Daily anger journal: "What would I have said today, to whom, in one truthful sentence?" Five minutes, every night. Doesn't have to be sent. The point is to thaw the channel by giving the suppressed signal a safe, repeated, low-stakes outlet.

**Aloneness Exposure and Social Redundancy Work — new tier-1 addition.**

This addresses the second driver named in Section 3.6.

- **Aloneness exposure**, in graded doses. Short solo trips (a weekend in nature, a solo sailing day, a solo restaurant dinner with no phone). The work is to build evidence that the state of aloneness is survivable and even, sometimes, generative. Most people who fear aloneness have never deliberately practiced being alone in a contained, chosen way; they only experience it as something happening to them.

Rarus + men's group + reactivated old friendship + one shared-activity group (sailing club, hiking group) + one therapist or close confidant. The principle is that no single relationship or project should be load-bearing for non-aloneness, because if it is, the system will resist any challenge to it (including challenges that are appropriate).

This addresses the wider expressive inhibition named in Section 3.7. The anger-recovery work in this Tier focuses on a specific sub-channel; this set focuses on the wider face-to-face register. They reinforce each other.

### Tier 2 (high value, supportive)

**Internal Family Systems (IFS) — still useful, not primary.**

(In plain terms: **IFS** treats the mind as composed of distinct *parts*, each with its own intentions, memories, and feelings. ## 12. The Single Most Important Reframe

The original self-report named the problem as "fear." That is the surface description and it is not entirely wrong — the felt experience really is "fear runs my life." But the data, accumulated across the Q&A and three addenda, names a more specific picture. There are **two drivers** sitting on top of a **temperamentally cautious substrate**, and the mechanism of expression is a **wider face-to-face inhibition** of which the closed anger channel is the most severely closed sub-channel.

> **Substrate (from birth): a behaviorally inhibited temperament.** Slow to warm to novelty. Higher autonomic reactivity to new settings. Not pathology; constitution. The work is not to remove this; it is to design around it and stop treating it as a defect.
>
> **Driver 1: The anger and confrontation-output sub-channel was closed at age 13–14**, on top of an already cautious temperament and an already conflict-avoidant family climate. The autonomic system still fires. The body still knows. The mind has been trained not to.
>
> **Driver 2: Fear of being alone is the deeper aversive that locks the avoidance pattern in place.** Stepping over yourself sometimes costs relationships; lost relationships move toward the state most feared; therefore the rule becomes "do not step over yourself," and the cultured-behavior surface is what that rule looks like in daily life.
>
> **Mechanism (wider than first appeared): the entire face-to-face expressive channel is dampened.** Anger is the most severely closed sub-channel. But warmth-shown, recognition-claimed, naming-the-other, holding-gaze, smiling-openly, marking-the-self-as-visible are also dampened. The body has the affect. The face has been holding it back for decades.

Everything follows from these layers stacked. The interpersonal-confrontation paralysis follows. The staying-too-long-in-bad-relationships follows. The choice of partners who bind tightly and demand little expressed warmth follows. The single-anchor attachment to Rarus Health follows. The somatic stress symptoms follow. The aversion to using names, to *папа* and *мама*, to eye contact, to open smiles, to rings and watches and chains — all of it is the same channel, shut from the inside, in the face-to-face moment. The eulogy shame line ("didn't reach anything of significance because he was always in fear to step over himself") follows: stepping over yourself requires (a) the kind of friction-with-the-world the closed anger channel is designed to avoid, AND (b) tolerance for the periods of aloneness that boldness sometimes creates, AND (c) the willingness to be visible and claimed in face-to-face moments rather than slipping past them.

The work is not to become less afraid. The work is **three parallel projects**:

1. **Reopen the anger channel** so the body's signal can finally arrive at the surface, where it can be felt, named, and converted into action — including the action of leaving conversations, relationships, and roles that should have ended years ago, and including the action of *staying* and saying the truthful sentence instead.
2. **Build tolerance for aloneness, and build social redundancy** so that no single relationship or project carries the full weight of "you will not be alone." Held tightly, things distort the choices around them. Held warmly, they become genuinely good.
3. **Reopen the wider face-to-face channel.** Use names. Hold gaze. Let the smile reach the face. Wear the ring. Say *папа* out loud. None of these is large on its own; together they reverse the ten thousand small avoidances that built the inhibition in the first place.

> *"I am afraid."*

The addenda let us extend that sentence in two directions:

> *"I am afraid. Specifically, I am afraid of being alone. And I am afraid of being seen."*

That sentence, said out loud, to the right person, while looking at them, with their name in it, is the first move.
