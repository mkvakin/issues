# Self-Examination (Rephrased in Clear English)

Now let me turn from the other side and list my own problems.

## The Core Pattern

The central problem is that I am **driven by my fears**. I cannot live a **meaningful life** because, somewhere underneath, I am always afraid of something.

For example, instead of doing genuinely interesting engineering work, I spent years grinding through new frameworks and chasing certifications, until I finally understood that road was a dead end.

## In Relationships

The same pattern shows up with women. I always exited too late, well after the signals to leave had piled up.

- **First wife**: she was already drinking heavily. It was clear nothing was working, and I had wanted out for a long time, but I kept postponing. Maybe because she was loyal. Maybe because I was afraid leaving would be very hard. Either way, fear was in the picture.
- **Second girlfriend**: it was already obvious she was manipulative, that she was extracting money, and that she had grown brazen. But I only left when a third woman appeared.
- **Third (current) wife**: in retrospect, I shouldn't have married her. It was visible early that she escalated fast, that she had **anger management problems**. But I felt awkward pulling out: I'd taken responsibility for her, and I needed her on my insurance, which required marriage. Then for many years, after each blow-up, I wanted out, but by then we had joint property, and so the relationship dragged on and on until she ended it herself.

## Career Choice as Avoidance

I suspect my lack of creativity is connected to the same pattern. A subconsciously frightened person is also afraid to transcend the boundaries of what he already knows.

When I entered engineering in 1987, I did it largely because Voenmeh got me out of the draft, and I didn't want to end up in Afghanistan. If I hadn't been thinking in those terms, more options that genuinely interested me might have surfaced.

So the question is: **am I unconsciously suppressing my options through fear?**

## Other Manifestations

- Reluctance to negotiate for equity in a company - is this the same thing?
- The fact that I get no real pleasure from skiing.
- The habit of postponing active pursuits and replacing them with "preparation for the future" that never arrives. A typical *intelligentsia* habit.

I wouldn't say this is fear of making mistakes - that one doesn't seem to be present. I can make a mistake without punishing myself for it.

And it's not accurate to say I'm a loser either. I made it to Silicon Valley, climbed to a meaningful position, and earned more than most of my classmates ever did.

## Deference to Social Norms

There is also a pull toward respecting social rules.

On one hand, that is healthy - respecting law and order. On the other hand, it looks like a strategy for avoiding personal conflict, hoping that some law or rule will resolve the matter on its own, so I never have to confront anyone directly.

Right now, for example, I should be preparing for divorce - gathering information, analyzing it, building a position. I cannot make myself do it. Or rather, I can, but only with enormous effort. And I know that doing this work would actually make things easier and reduce my chances of losing.

## Origin: Around Age 13-14

My grandmother once said: "Behave so that other people also find it comfortable to interact with you." I liked it immediately, and it landed on fertile ground. Another person might not have noticed it, but I did. So I was already drawn in that direction; it served me.

Up to about age 13 or 14, I don't remember having this background sense of being afraid of something. Maybe I just didn't understand myself well. But then the fear appeared rather quickly, and I started seeking out rules - perhaps reframing it as "being civilized" - without real necessity. Until age 14 I fought freely in class; then very abruptly I started avoiding fights altogether.

Probably no outsider would notice these patterns; most people don't notice them in themselves either.

## Why It Now Hurts

In an industrial society, where everyone quietly climbs the career ladder at their factory, this would not have hurt me. I'd have made it to some middle-management position and everything would have been fine.

But in a modern life, where you have to make sharp decisions - moving between countries, deciding whether to sell or buy real estate, deciding when to sell stocks - this pattern becomes deeply counterproductive and seriously poisons life, because life starts to feel meaningless.

To end up a loser with normal capabilities is just stupid.

## How the Fear Shows Up

In most situations I don't experience visceral, gut-dropping fear. It's more a skill of pushing the fear deep into the subconscious, where it then quietly directs my actions from below.

## Other Personality Notes

- **Absorption**: I can become deeply absorbed in things like agentic coding, sit with them for hours.
- **Resistance to chores** until I force myself.
- **Strong introversion**. I wouldn't call it ADHD, but my attention itself decides what I will work on.
- **I read books slowly**. That is also a trait of mine.
